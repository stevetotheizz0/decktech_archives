Note from Editor. The card reviews will now be based on a 5 star rating loosely adapted from the Inquest star rating. I like it because it can be defined so well.

***** Five stars - These are the cards you see in tournaments time and time again. They are the best at what they need to do. They can fit in nearly any deck, and generally have excellent stand-alone value. ex. Darth Vader

**** Four stars - These are also very good cards, but often are a notch below the quality of a five star card, or lose a lot of effectiveness without supporting cards. Often seen in tournaments, but generally in specific deck types. ex. How did we get into this mess?

*** Three stars - These are average cards. They may be important in very specific deck types, but generally have few outstanding capabilites and are not often seen in constructed decks, or are used mostly when an expansion first comes out. Often can be useful in a sealed deck. ex. astromech droids.

** Two stars - A poor quality card. They are either too slow, too expensive, or have some kind of prohibitive drawback that makes them not very useful. Often there are other cards that fulfill similar functions, but much better. Almost never seen in a constructed deck, but in a sealed deck they might make it. ex. Nikto

* One star - A useless card. Often serves no particular use, or is very easily disrupted. Never seen at tournaments. ex. Ghoel

Introduction:

DBO is an objective card from special edition.

Text:

Front:

Deploy Dantooine System

While this side up, once during each of your deploy phases, may deploy from reserve deck to Dantooine one site or non-unique Rebel; Reshuffle. At Dantooine locations, each Imperial is deploy +2.

Flip this card if Rebels control at least three Dantooine sites and opponent controls no Dantooine locations.

Place out of play if Dantooine is "Blown Away".

Back:

While this side up, opponent's Force Drains are -1. At Dantooine locations, each Imperial is deploy +2. Your squadrons may deploy to Dantooine (deploy cost=squadrons power -3), are immune to attrition < 4 and may draw one battle destiny if not able to otherwise. Your force drains are +1 at systems where you have a squadron present.

Flip this card if opponent controls at least two Dantooine locations.

Place out of play if Dantooine is "Blown Away".

Summary.

_____Dbo, like any complex card, has its strengths and weaknesses. I'll start with the strengths. Opponents force drains are -1 are very powerful, and imperials deploy +2 at dantooine sites helps secure the ground. Also, Dantooine is a fairly secure starting location, and starting with a planet can have many advantages, such as starting with nightfall or having a planet out to put clouds on. You can also deploy non-unique sites or rebels from the reserve deck, also a fairly useful ability, as you can quickly flush out your zero destiny draws and set up a force base early on. However, the advantages you get when the objective flips are not really that powerful, other than the force drain -1. Squadrons have a deployment restriction at Dantooine, hampering their combat usefullness. Force drains +1 at systems with squadrons could be useful, but remember the card has to be flipped to deploy them or get a drain bonus. And here-in I think lies the biggest drawback.

_____Dbo is pretty easily disrupted. It is not that hard to flip back over. The opponent need only control 2 Dantooine locations, be they non-unique locations of his or cloud sectors, or your own ground locations. And once Dbo is flipped over, it is very difficult to flip back. First of all, you have to spread out a fair amount on the ground, which is always dangerous. And if you are planning on playing space, it is to say the least, very difficult, to balance a ground deck, and a space deck. That being said, though, occupy the ground and one or two squadrons at kessel, kiffex and coruscant(drains of 4, 3 and 4) will spell quick doom for an opponent, even with force drains -1.

_____Furthermore, other deck types can perform similar operations better, namely Local Uprising / Liberation. You still get the force drains -1, a matching operative is worth any 5 non-unique rebels you can name, you get force retrieval, locations come out while the objective is flipped, force drain +1 at sites with operatives, it is almost impossible to flip back over, etc,etc. And if you're really into a space overpower deck, Hidden Base will get you out fast (although i really question the effectiveness of hidden base after the errata). Finally, although it's obviously not in direct competion, Ralltiir Operations, is way better. Compare - being able to deploy squadrons, as opposed to pulling out any card from the reserve deck!!!(endless demonic tutor), and adding to your battle destinies everywhere.

_____One final note, although this card seems not so hot now, it may change. If Decipher again reverses the ruling on Ryle Torsyn being able to add a drain to every Dantooine location (add 1 to each of your force drains at dantooine, strange wording) then I think this deck could be very viable for a quick spread out and drain. I'm looking at maximum drains of around 18 a turn with that.

Conclusion:

_____Dbo's force drains -1 are definitely the strongest part of this card, keeping it from a one star rating. However the ineffectiveness of it, especially compared to other objectives and deck types, makes it less than useful.

Review by Eric Olson

Subject of Review: Dantooine Base Operations

Overall rating: ** (two stars) (note from editor - Eric originally gave a 3 out of 10 based on the intoductory 10 point scale. I am basically cutting it in half, and fitting it into the criteria mentioned at the top of the page.)

_____In short, this card is nearly binder fodder because it breaks one of the

cardinal rules of tourney level decks, don't play heavy space AND heavy

ground. In order to gain the benefits, you have to be playing large

quantities of both systems and squadrons in order to get enough of them

out, as well as maintaining a large ground presence in order to prevent

flipping.

_____But let's take a look at another reason why this card earns such a low

rating. The main benefit is it lets your squadrons deploy, but wait,

they can only deploy to Dantooine. What this means is the majority of

your air force may only deploy to one system. In other words, you won't

win any major space battles because you have little 'beatdown' capacity.

_____Most of the other benefits this card gives is better done by other

cards. Here's the list:

1. Opponent's force drains are -1.

Generic operatives provides this bonus, and is a heck of a lot easier to

keep flipped.

2. Immunity to attrition < 4 and battle destiny if not able to

otherwise.

This provides the battle bonus for this deck. If you want to do

battling in space, use Hidden Base - Xwing swarm with Organized Attack.

This provides far more battling bonus than this meager bonus.

_____Alternatively, generic operatives are better than this, because they ADD

a battle destiny, and with a dozen in the deck, who cares that they're

not immune to any attrition.

3. Force drains are +1 with a squadron.

Again, generic operatives provide this, and as a bonus, you never have

to flip the objective

_____Alternatively, EBO gives a bonus of +2 to drains, and provides battle

bonuses to boot, and is only marginally harder to activate if you build

your deck around it, and much harder to cancel.

4. Imperials are deploy +2 to Dantooine.

Not bad, but it's only to support the above weak bonuses.

5. Once per turn, may deploy a non-unique rebel or generic site to

Dantooine.

_____The saving grace, as it provides a limited 'cheese' theme of deploying

rebel cadets and being able to play numbers, especially good in concert

with 4 above.

_____Now that we've looked at the benefits, let's look at the requirements.

To flip, we must control 3 sites with rebels, while preventing opponent

from controlling any locations. This is probably not too difficult with

the +2 deploy for Imps, so I'll concede an easy initial flip.

To keep flipped, we must prevent the opponent from controlling two

locations. Obviously, the opponent will not attempt to control the

system, so let's just deal with the sites. With your forces split

between two fronts, you're basically playing with 1/2 a ground deck. A

dedicated dark ground deck has a 'full' ground deck to deal with your

forces. Even given the 'terrain' bonuses from the obvious generic sites

of Forest/Swamp/Jungle, what are the odds of preventing a take over from

the ground deck? Slim to none.

Some closing thoughts:

_____A dedicated space deck will prey on your deployment restrictions to

Dantooine, and be able to choose the places for major battles to take a

place, thus minimizing your space bonuses.

A dedicated ground deck will virtually always be able to flip the

objective back due to your limited ground power.

A dedicated clouds deck will drop two clouds on Dantooine and slaughter

you in the clouds should you attempt a challenge.

--

Thanks,

Eric

Card Review: Dantooine Base Operations/More Dangerous Than You Realize

Travis John "Salacious Crumb" Willse

sal-crumb@geocities.com

_____Dantooine Base Operations (R, Special Edition), arguably the least

played of all 10 objectives currently in the game, is primarily an

objective designed to enhance a rarely seen card subtype: squadrons.

Squadrons, despite their high permanent pilot ability, cost-efficient

movement and overall bang per card slot, are difficult to deploy (even

with the advent of DBO), do not offer the movement flexibility of

individual starships, and are prime targets for the arsenal of Dark

starship weapons. An SFS L-s9.3 Laser Cannons (C, Special Edition) needs

to draw only a 1 for destiny to cause a squadron of Y-wings to be

immediately lost (a hefty loss). Squadron cards themselves are also

moderately difficult to obtain-the only two available for the Light Side

are X-wing Assault Squadron (R, Special Edition) and Y-wing Assault

Squadron (U1, A New Hope). Despite these problems, DBO remains a card

of high potential.

_____Like its Dark sister Ralltiir Operations (R, Special Edition), DBO

forces you to start with its corresponding system, and lets you deploy

sites or non-unique Rebels from Reserve Deck to that system. To aid in

fulfilling the moderately difficult goal of controlling three Dantooine

(U2, Premiere) sites with Rebels (while not allowing your opponent to

control any), DBO has the added bonus of making Imperials deploy +2 to

Dantooine locations.

_____The flip side, however, is markedly different from its "twin." Aside

from the powerful Force drain reduction (and retaining the added deploy

cost for the opponent), DBO casts off many of the shackles that restrict

squadrons. Rather than Ralltiir Ops' "search for any card" text, DBO

lets X-wing and Y-wing squadrons deploy for 6 and 3 Force, respectively,

to Dantooine (both great deploy-forfeit ratios), are granted immunity to

attrition < 4, guaranteed battle destiny draws and Force drain bonuses

where present.

_____Most Dark decks (even pure space decks) can't handle this kind of power.

Confrontation is much easier for the Light side with this cheap,

recyclable space power. On the off-chance that you do have to lose a

squadron in battle, merely place it on a Docking And Repair Facilities

(R, Special Edition) for a high-power card that, because of its

asterisk-defined deploy cost, returns to your hand for free. It gets

even better with cards like Incom Engineer (C, Special Edition),

Organized Attack (C, Special Edition) and S-foils (C, Special Edition).

Using the latter two of those makes each of your X-wing Assault

Squadrons deploy 6, forfeit 12 and power 18. For defense against the

rare weapon-laden TIE decks, A Few Maneuvers (C, Premiere) is an

excellent high-destiny defensive measure. If the opponent is weak in

space, or merely avoids you, take advantage with high drain systems. A

flipped DBO with squadrons at Coruscant (R, Special Edition) and Kessel

(U2, Premiere) will net you drains of 4 at each of those locations.

Adding a squadron-controlled Kiffex (R, Jabba's Palace) gives a total

drain of 11 per turn-amazing for a deck style not part of the modern

metagame in most places. Other space cards will mix well with DBO:

Asteroid Sanctuary (C, Dagobah) and Kessel Run (R2, Premiere). Or, throw

in A Gift (U, Jabba's Palace) and Artoo (R, Jabba's Palace) and deploy

one of your compatible generic sites to your Jabba's Palace: Audience

Chamber (U, Jabba's Palace) to reduce all of your opponent's Force

drains by 2. For true DBO decks, a 50/50 mix of space and ground (with

a number of starfighters to provide pre-flip space power) should provide

sufficient power in both places to create give a high probability of

winning.

_____DBO also works well in several decks where you do not intend to flip it.

Its always functional deploy +2 cost for Imperials has great potential.

By replacing Bothawui Operatives (C, Special Edition) with Ithorians (C,

Jabba's Palace), it can be used as an alternate objective for setting up

a Droid Merchant (C, Special Edition) engine, a clouds deck (which

actually mixes well with squadrons) or a Nabrun Leids / Beatdown deck.

The cost increase also serves insert decks well, whose characters are

typically weaker than others. For added protection, throw in Goo Nee

Tay! (R, Special Edition) and H'nemthes (C, Jabba's Palace). With

these cards in effect, Imperial pilots of ability > 2 are a prohibitive

deploy +6 to Dantooine, while most other regularly used Dark side

characters (save a few specialized aliens) are a restrictive deploy +2

or +4 to those sites.

_____In conclusion, Dantooine Base Operations is an underrated card that, if

you have the resources, makes an excellent alternative deck style often

not considered by other.

Overall rating: **** (changed from original 8 out of 10- editor)

SWCCG Tournament Report-3/14/99

On Sunday, March 14, 1999, 11 players met at Homefront Hobbies in Williamsport, PA for a Bespin & Beyond Swiss Tournament. Two new players also attended to learn how to play the game. I am hoping to help them construct decks so that they will be able to play at the next tournament in 2 weeks. Thanks to all those players who donated their unwanted cards to these two new players.

I decided to try some new prize distribution methods, which included "Bingo" prizes with posters, stickers, and a pack for the lowest ranked game. I opened the 7 packs of Japanese cards, and found both a LUKE SKYWALKER and OBI'S LIGHTSABER!!! I saved those two for special prizes at the end of the

day, and had players choose a couple of the Japanese cards every time they completed one game. The $3 entry fee was used to purchase prizes from the store owner at a nice discount. Thanks Brian!!

We had two undefeated players, who never faced each other as they were both playing the same allegiance for the final game. Congratulations to Ben Hughes, who took first place, using both a LS and a DS Operative deck. Bryan Westerby was also undefeated, with a lower differential. While his DS deck

did use the Operative Objective, his LS deck did not contain an Objective, relying instead on the raw power associated with 19 Sandspeeders on steroids. Chad Chervinsky rose above the crowd to take 3rd place, his best showing ever.

A. Sujdak's on "How to solve for the assymetric case"

 

____In previous ramblings by A. Sujdak, a systematic framework of assumptions was provided for exploring metagames of a symmetric type. Below is a brief sketch of things to come in my research of how to solve for the assymetric case, and also a pseudo proof of the solubility of an assymetric meta.

____Let the lowercase w after a decktype denote that decktype's aggregate winning percentage--i.e., its winning percentage if it were to play onegame against each opponent in the entire field. Also let the lowercase pafter a decktype denote its proportion of the total field. Let the light side decktypes be represented by L1, L2, etc. and let the dark side decktypes be represented by D1, D2, etc. Lastly, let the metagame winning percentage matrix be represented byM[a,b], where the value of the matrix at any particular coordinate is the percentage of time that decktype a defeats decktype b.

____Unlike A. Sujdak's first Equilibrium Theory, it must be additionally assumed that players do not take expected margins of victory into account when they decide which deck to play. We then arrive at the following system of linear equations:

LNw = Lw for all N

DIw = Dw for all I

____This is an extension of the normal equilibrium theory. It can no longer be assumed that decks are bound to a fifty percent winning percentage, because it is possible to have a stable meta in which light (dark) wins morethan dark (light). The new restriction is simply that all decks on the sameside of the force must have the same winning percentage. If this were not true then the number of people playing the deck with the best winning percentage characteristics would increase and either it would be hosed and dragged back down to the same win character as the other decktypes or no counter would exist in which case all players would play that decktype. These sets of restrictions give (total number of decktypes) minus two equations.

sum(LNp) over N = 1

and

sum(DIp) over I = 1

____This gives two more equations which basically define the decktype populations as representing that percentage of the field which plays a particular decktype. The restriction obviously comes from the fact that as more people play one decktype the number of people playing other decktypes on that side of the force must decrease.

sum(M[ i, j ] * Ljp) over j = Diw and sum(M[ i, j] * Djp) = Liw

____This gives one equation for each decktype basically stating that the aggregate win percentage for a deck is a linear summation of its winning percentages against opposing decktypes times their frequency in themeta. This system of equations gives two equations for each decktype. Since we have two descriptors for each decktype, aggregate winning percentage and population, we have arrived at a solvable system of equations (assuming that none of the boundedness restrictions is violated, in which case asolution is still possible but will be dynamic and non-deterministic in nature).

____The real trick in arriving at a succesful adaptation of Eq. Theory was to find a way to state the equilibrium condition. It took a fair amount of time on the part of the author to see the new winning conditions. I then nearly overlooked the population restriction equations and was ready to write an article stating that assymetric metas were insoluble! Fortuneatly, I developed a brain at the last minute and you have the masterpiece(hehehe) that you see before you. Honestly, I'm not sure how easy it will be to obtain a deterministic solution but I'm hoping for the best.

I haven't had much time to probe into empirical validation, either, because I had a birthday yesterday. Surprise parties ruin your work plans.

In any case, to give some sort of real-world applicability to what you see above, I see the current metagame as consisting of the following:

LS: HB X-wing, Maelstrom, Bothan Ops

DS: ISGhhhk, SFS, HDADTJ

____I know that multiple variants exist of each of these decktypes, but they have similiar performance characteristics. Furthermore I do not believe that there is a significant population of decks that exist completely outside the above meta. With three general decktypes on each side of the force, all that really needs to be done is to find M, the win matrix,and it SHOULD be possible to predict the stable population of each decktype. With a relatively simple meta like above it will be interesting to see how well the theory agrees with the empirical findings (i.e., global popularities of each of the different decktypes).

Jim Li "Traffic Control and Reactor Terminal"

 

____Recently on the Listserve I've heard a little debate going back and forth about the benefits of Reactor Terminal/Traffic Control. Some people put these cards in every deck because they help get unwanted cards out of your hand, recycle high destiny, protect you from Monnok/Grimtassh etc. While others argue that these 2 cards give you a false sense of safety and allow you to overdraw in order to find them, and wreck you if you don't.

____I haven't put a Reactor Terminal in my deck for quite a few months. Traffic control made a cameo in my Dagobah training deck due to the high number of useless destiny 7s in that deck, but it hasn't been in any of my other decks. I follow the belief that you should never draw up so many cards that not having a Traff/Term will leave you with very little force. I always try to keep my hand under 12 cards when possible. Not having those cards hasn't really affected my games that much.

Secret Tech:

____I first saw an Ishi Tib being played by another highly skilled player in my area. For those of you who don't know what an ishi tib does, it is an LS non-unique (3 dots) alien, power and ability = 1 (but armor = 3) that deploys for 2. Destiny 3, and during your draw phase, allows you to put one card from you hand at the bottom of your used pile, and draw a card. At first glance a pretty useless card, but if you play with it for a while, you realize that its ability is awesome. In my decks that don't contain Traffic Control, this card is almost always in and serves the same functions. It helps protect me from monnok, allows me to recycle that useless Don't forget the droids, etc. About the only thing that it doesn't do is boost my differential at the end of a game.

____Term/Control also serves a different purpose. In a tournament game, it allows you to put your entire hand in your used pile at the end of the game to boost your differential. This is a really attractive part of Term/control, and has helped many players needing differential, but I'll tell you why I don't use it. First, if you win all of your games, differential matters very little. While I realize that this is not always possible, I try my hardest to win first and worry about differential later. Second, you opponent, if they are a tournament veteran, will pick up on what you're trying to do and simply draw up a turn before you drain them to death, making Term/control pointless. Third, and the deciding factor for me, is that when I'm making a deck, there are always a couple more "cool" cards that I want to throw in,and this doesn't leave any room for recyclers.

____I hope this article has been somewhat helpful. I guess I am of the opinion that Traffic Control and Reactor Terminal aren't necessary in most decks and can be replaced by more useful cards.

John Arendt "Hunt Down and Destroy the Jedi"

 

Introduction:

____A recent concern in the Star Wars community has been the rise of decks that use swarms of non-unique cards, getting away from Main characters and the "spirit of Star Wars". Likewise, many current deck designs are non-interactive, and focus on force draining the opponent to death while making it difficult to battle and do damage (Operatives, ISB Operations, Hidden Base). Heres an aggressive deck that makes use of some Mains and takes the fight to the opponent. Its probably not the design I would take to a major tournament, but it does extremely well in local play. More importantly, its a design that emphasizes Main characters and encourages the dark side to battle. An analysis of the deck elements follows the deck list.

Locations:

____The idea behind this deck is to play against your opponent, preferably on their locations. My area is rife with Operatives, and most Operative decks provide 2 force on your first turn, allowing the 6 force necessary to drop Vader on their starting location (which is generally the Farm) and flip the Objective on the first turn.

____The locations are geared towards safe activation - the Jabbas Palace sites and Imperial Holotable are all immune to Revolution. In an area where Revolution is heavily played, I would increase the number of Evaders in the deck and add more locations for force generation. Currently, the 7 sites is enough, as Operatives and Hidden Base tend to provide quite a bit of quick force to the Dark Side. Masterful Move and the Jabbas Palace game text also allow some sites to come out of the deck. Dont forget the additional activation at the Holotheater once Vader is on the table.

Characters:

____Its important early in the game to read the opponents deck and come up with a game plan. Against Operatives, I tend to deploy Vader immediately, and then begin developing hit squads - Vader/Tarkin, Vader/Evazan, Boba Fett/Evazan, Chall Bekan/Boelo, Jabba/Boelo, Blizzard 2/ORS, etc - there are many combinations of these characters that are effective in battle.

Green and Blue Cards:

____This is certainly not enough ships to effectively battle a Hidden Base deck. However, it is possible to probe with 4 ships, especially under the new HB rules. Bossk in Hounds tooth is key because of the added destiny.

Interrupts:

____The interrupts are geared towards defeating a non-unique deck. Alter and Monnok are the main ingredients; get rid of pitch effects (ie, Bargaining Table) and Yarna, and then go to town with the Monnoks. Against an Operative deck, the constant pressure of Vader draining and battling on the Operative planet, along with Visage loss and Monnok, is overwhelming. I would employ a similar strategy vs Hidden Base - use speed to begin the damage early, bolstered by Monnok. As the opponent spreads to make Hidden Base active, he becomes vulnerable to some of the light space elements in the deck.

____As for other interrupts, Shocking Revelation will be discussed in conjunction with Secret Plans (below). Holonet Transmission protects the Visage against Termination and makes an additional hologram for the Holotable. Torture is anti-inserts - I find that whenever you assume you wont face any inserts and take out your insert protection, you play against all numbers decks. Its a law. Torture and Dont Forget the Droids belong in most Mains decks.

Effects:

____I start with Secret Plans. Not only does retrieval overcome the early fast damage that Visage inflicts, but the ability to pull Shocking Revelation (and recycle it) each turn is very useful. The keys to getting this deck moving fast are: Vader in the opening hand and a battleground site to deploy him. If either of these elements are not present, the recycling Shock can let you deduce the contents of your active force pile, allowing you to judiciously draw up force or leave it (of course, you need to know the deck contents well, but that should be a given). This maximizes the decisions you make early in the game.

____For instance, if I dont have a Vader in the opening hand or force pile, I may look to the next option perhaps an early Monnok. Likewise if the opponent starts Goo Nee Tay to slow Vader down a little.

____The other effect that bears a close look at is Broken Concentration. Against a training deck, of course this is a useful starting effect. However, it also warrants a card slot as a defensive card. The pay to move a force back onto opponents force pile is extremely effective against cards that require force to be saved up - Its a Trap, Quite a Mercenary, Whatre You Trying to Push on Us, especially when you have prior knowledge of the opponents hand with a Monnok and know what the force is being saved up for at that point, drop Broken Concentration and go to town.

____The second Visage is important if your original is cancelled; the steady Visage damage is a constant poke, and will be the deciding factor in a long, defensive game. Because of the popularity of Transmission Terminated, I tend to pull Holonet Transmission early on with a Masterful Move as added protection.

Conclusion:

____This deck has a lot of room for variance based on your local tournament scene. As mentioned, this version is designed to face off against Operatives, and to provide an interactive, aggressive experience beating on those nasty little shadows on the Operative planet. Character removal in battle (using Vaders choke, Evazans game text, or You Are Beaten) is useful in avoiding extra battle destinies provided by the Operative or an Epp combo. Some improvements would be making room for an additional Elis Helrot, or adding another grabber card, like Therell Be Hell to Pay. Against a field with a lot of X Wing swarm decks, add a Ghhhk to be pulled out with Masterful Move.

 

 

David Destefanis "The Defensive Strategy"

 

____The Defensive player is the nemesis of the Offensive player: he first build defenses, then attack his opponent when he feels/is safe from any countermeasure. This is somehow much more difficult to do efficiently than to jump early on your opponent to kill him. Thus, good and winning defending decks are somewhat rare in the tournament scene. But since the first players fear is to lose, many tend to begin their tournament experience as Defensive players.

____In the end, an usual SW:CCG player has decks with a versatile and general defense capability and a much more pronounced offense part (which will result to be his deck strategy).

What are the basis of the Defensive Deck ?

1) Locations: Defensive decks dont have that many location, so you wont see a Def. Deck with 10 locations. This is because the more locations you put inside your deck, the more your opponent will be able to drain you if these are on table; then, you waste precious card space better used elsewhere. The average number is between 6 to 8.

2) Characters/starships/vehicles : here there is also an upper limit, because the deck purpose is not to control every location in the game, but that doesnt mean that a defensive deck has a lower average number of ch/st/veh, just that they will be very different from an usual type of deck, with roles pretty much defined within the decks strategy.

3) Interrupts/Effects: these are the core components of the Defensive deck, and range from pure defensive ones, like Wars Not Make One Great/Narrow Escape to more nasty damaging ones like Uncontrollable Fury/Surprise Assault. Most Defensive decks also count on regeneration methods like Kessel Run/Off The Edge, since it is likely that in the overall the Defensive deck will be dealt more damage than the opponents one.

4) At least 1 offensive tactic to damage the opponent (since the Time Win isnt what players are looking for).

5) A well, thoroughly studied game plan. If you stay on the defensive, you let players develop their strategies, so you had better have a solid game plan if you are to survive to the opponents constant attentions. Thus, a Defensive deck needs much more refining than other decks.

What are the basis of a GOOD Defensive Deck ?

____Method 1: the Defensive deck doesnt care what the opponent do, since it will be able to recover from even the sturdiest attack with Force Retrieval. This usually means a good retrieval method, the more foolproof possible, and a heavy one also. These decks usually concentrate on their retrieval capability and damage the opponent in an also safe way (and slow, like Holo-drain). Just why they prefer slow and safe methods is not really difficult to guess: these decks usually cannot spare more than a few card slots to pure offensive strategies, so the player doesnt want to gamble on the security of those cards.

An example of an average method 1 deck would be the All Droid/Numbers with Off The Edge. In this case, the defending player will have to protect Artoo from destruction (here go a few interrupts) to play Off The Edge on Cloud City, will have to somehow counter huge drain with Surprise Assaults, and damage the opponent with insert effects (and Surprise Assault) while making them appear on the top of the opponents Reserve Deck with cards such as CC: Sabaac (use Retractable Arm on a R2-unit), Beru Stew, Blue Milk, Recoil In Fear, or Wars Not Make One Great. The main problem, however, is the gamble factor: the Surprise Assault may work once, but it is not a foolproof tactic, and insert effects dont tell you if they are going to show at the top of your opponents deck before you are dead; also, counter exist. This deck is too much unreliable to be useful at top-level competition.

A good example of a good method one deck would be the Mind What Have You Learned/Save You It Can deck. Yes, this is the famous deck nobody plays but everyone wants to hear about (or at least see a good one). The deck, with all its data, will be posted separately.

____Method 2: the Defensive decks cares what the opponent do, and change its tactic accordingly. This kind of deck deliberately leaves the initiative to the opponent and mold itself to partially/totally shut it down, and then damage him where he is not so well prepared to have a confrontation. In this case, the Defensive deck has riskier offensive methods than the method 1 deck, because he has more cards on his offensive strategy list and knows the opponent has already spent most of his elsewhere. So when this decks kicks in, he will damage the other one badly each turn without too much fear of retaliation, while blocking the others drains nearly everywhere else. The Baragwin deck was a perfect pre-SE example of a good method 2 deck, but other could also be more fight-oriented, without retrieval methods but more characters/spies/landing claws/starships. Narrow Escape is a card to build decks upon, now that S/A/C are no more so predominant.

____Method 3: it is a much more personal method/approach. Before continuing on, lets see together what you can possibly do in a SW: CCG game, and what would you like to be able to do:

1) You would like to know the composition of the opponents deck, and possibly in the details. It may be crucial to know if your opponent has or has not a particular counter(s) to your strategy.

2) You would like to be able to have all your needed cards in hand. This doesnt rely only on luck.

3) You would like to be protected from any nasty/broken/predominant deck strategy out there.

4) You would like to be lucky. J Well, this is scientifically impossible to achieve, but the Force is here, believe me

5) You could heavily damage your opponent.

6) You could quickly and without much risks retrieve Force.

7) You would like to win with huge differentials most of the time, so as not to worry about differential issues in tournament.

8) The Force is your ally, so the less you squander, the best it would be, but is that possible ? This may seems a card efficiency problem, but there is more than meets the eye.

9) You would like to have many different nasty cards/tricks, but also to be somewhat sure to play them during the game.

10) You would like to have fun and have a challenge.

11) (And last) You would like to see your opponent lose by his own hands. (This is a more personal opinion)

____Well, all the last 11 points are possible to accomplish, but usually not all 11 together (if you still want to win).

____Strange as it may seems, my DDMs LS Deck accomplishes them all. As I won the 98 Chandrilla Regional thanks to it, this is not idle chatter. This is what I call a close-to-perfection method 3 deck. Method 3 decks are decks that escape the bounds of logic, and so work with the same efficiency nearly all the time, and also seem to take advantage from the opponent knowing its composition. (I would even think they work best (=safely) when the opponent already dealt with it and lost). The DDMs LS Deck will also be posted elsewhere (for space issues).

____Conclusion: The Defensive Option is maybe, on the paper, the strongest one. But games arent won on Defense alone. So each Defensive deck has to contain some Offensive elements. The reverse is not true, however, and this is what makes Offensive decks (in general) more efficient: they are quicker, nastier, and usually card-efficient. Now, with all the different deck types out there (and with all the different viable offensive strategies), it is much more difficult to be ready to face them all.

____Even so, Defensive decks usually have always the means to win a particular game. To find out, usually, players use a term: the "turning point". The turning point happens when the Defensive deck kicks in: i.e. when the player manages to get his strategy going. At this point, the Defensive player usually is able to mount a come back through Force retrieval or to block damage to his deck altogether. This usually means the end of the opponents chances of winning. But the turning point is not easy to accomplish (especially in SE), because of the possible early damage the Defensive deck could take. Somehow, especially if the damage is high, it is possible for the Defensive deck to lose some crucial cards to its strategy and not to be able to retrieve them afterwards.

____This is why Defensive decks tend to lose in harsh tournaments. Another reason is that the same defending players are "bored" to see that they have to quicken their deck to reach the turning point (which usually means less deck versatility in the overall defense). But defensive decks are not dead, and they will continue to appear on the tournament scene.
